Selection for Litter Size and Litter Birthweight in Large White Pigs

Maximum, Mean and Variability of Reproduction Traits

Author: Ewa Sell-Kubiak

Institution: Department of Genetics and Animal Breeding, Poznan University of Life Sciences, Poland

Journal: Animal 15 (2021) 100352

View Original Paper

Paper Overview & Context

Citation: Sell-Kubiak, E. (2021). Selection for litter size and litter birthweight in Large White pigs: Maximum, mean and variability of reproduction traits. Animal, 15(9), 100352.

Context: Danish sows increased litter size from 11.7 to 17.5 piglets (2000-2019), creating management challenges including lactation capacity limitations, decreased birthweight, and increased stillborn rates.

Dataset: 23,193 Large White sows from Topigs Norsvin with 152,282 litter records, requiring ≥5 parities per sow with 1st parity recorded.

Key Innovation: Balanced Selection Strategy

This study introduces a comprehensive framework for balancing litter size optimization with trait uniformity by analyzing maximum, mean, and variability phenotypes simultaneously, providing practical breeding solutions for sustainable pig production.

Why, How, What Analysis
WHY - Management & Welfare Challenges
Increasing Litter Size Challenges:
  • Lactation capacity - Number of teats vs. piglet number mismatch
  • Decreasing birthweight - Larger litters produce smaller piglets
  • Higher stillborn rates - Welfare issues for sows and piglets
  • Management complexity - Difficulty handling very large litters
Biological Limitations:
  • Uterine capacity - Physical limits to reproduction
  • Trait variability - Increased variation with genetic improvement
  • Correlated responses - Positive correlation between mean and variance
HOW - Comprehensive Trait Analysis
Three-Trait Framework:
1. Maximum Traits (max)
  • Definition: Highest value from ≥5 records per sow
  • Purpose: Assess maximum uterine capacity
  • Traits: maxTNB, maxNBA, maxLW
  • Advantage: Higher heritability (0.24-0.31)
2. Mean Traits (mean)
  • Definition: Average across all parities per sow
  • Purpose: Assess consistent performance
  • Traits: meanTNB, meanNBA, meanLW
  • Challenge: Lower heritability (0.05-0.08)
3. Variability Traits (LnVar)
  • Definition: Log-transformed variance of residuals
  • Purpose: Assess trait uniformity
  • Traits: LnVarTNB, LnVarNBA, LnVarLW
  • Innovation: Genetic control of trait variation
Statistical Models:

Mixed-effect models in ASReml 4.1 with genetic, permanent environmental, and residual effects, including weighting factors for unequal records per sow.

WHAT - Key Findings & Recommendations
Heritability Results:
  • Maximum traits: h² = 0.24-0.31 (high response potential)
  • Mean traits: h² = 0.05-0.08 (slower response)
  • Variability traits: h² = 0.004-0.007 (limited but possible)
Critical Correlations:
  • Mean-Variability: rg = 0.66-0.74 (strong positive)
  • Cross-trait: rg = 0.33-0.99 (highly variable)
  • Implication: Increased size → increased variation
Breeding Recommendations:
  • Balanced approach: Consider mean + variability simultaneously
  • Maximum traits: Use for identification, not direct selection
  • Sow longevity: 15% reach maximum after parity 6
Mathematical Models & Methods
1
Classical Reproduction Traits Model
Base Model for TNB, NBA, LW:
y = Xβ + Za + Upe + e
Where:
• y = vector of observations
• X, Z, U = incidence matrices
• β = fixed effects (farm-year-season, parity, age)
• a = additive genetic effects
• pe = permanent environmental effects
• e = residual effects
Fixed Effects:
  • Farm-year-season of farrowing (1,435 levels)
  • Parity of sow (10 levels)
  • Age at first insemination
2
Maximum Value Traits Model
Model for maxTNB, maxNBA, maxLW:
y₁ = X₁β₁ + Z₁a₁ + e₁
Key Differences:
• Single record per sow (highest value)
• No permanent environmental effect
• Farm-year-season (1,265 levels)
• Effects recorded at maximum value occurrence
Selection Criteria:

First occurrence of maximum value used if multiple parities achieved the same maximum (following Freyer, 2018 approach).

3
Mean Value Traits Model
Model for meanTNB, meanNBA, meanLW:
y₂ = X₂β₂ + Z₂a₂ + e₂
Weighting System:
• Residuals weighted by number of litters per sow
• Accounts for unequal information per animal
• Farm-year-season of first farrowing (1,007 levels)
• Age at first insemination (meanLW only)
Calculation:
meanTrait = Σ(observations per sow) / n
4
Variability Traits Model
Two-Step Process: Step 1: Residual Calculation

Obtain residuals from classical model (1) for each observation

Step 2: Variance Calculation & Log-Transformation
LnVar = ln(Var(residuals per sow))
Model for LnVarTNB, LnVarNBA, LnVarLW:
y₃ = X₃β₃ + Z₃a₃ + e₃
Fixed Effects:
• Farm-year-season of first farrowing (1,007 levels)
• Weighted residuals (number of observations per sow)
• Unique variability value per sow per trait
5
Genetic Coefficient of Variation (GCVSDe)
Transformation from Variance to SD Scale:
GCVSDe = (σ²ₐᵥ(σ²ₑ)/σ²ₑ) × 0.5σₐᵥ
Purpose:
• Translate variance-level estimates to SD scale
• Assess potential response to selection
• Compare across different traits
• Practical interpretation for breeders
Results:
  • LnVarTNB: GCVSDe = 0.080 (8% potential change)
  • LnVarNBA: GCVSDe = 0.063 (6% potential change)
  • LnVarLW: GCVSDe = 0.087 (9% potential change)
6
Multivariate Correlation Analysis
Trivariate Models for Mean Traits:
[y_meanTNB] [X_meanTNB 0 0 ] [β_meanTNB] [y_meanNBA] = [0 X_meanNBA 0 ] [β_meanNBA] + [y_meanLW ] [0 0 X_meanLW] [β_meanLW ] [Z_meanTNB 0 0 ] [a_meanTNB] [e_meanTNB] [0 Z_meanNBA 0 ] [a_meanNBA] + [e_meanNBA] [0 0 Z_meanLW] [a_meanLW ] [e_meanLW ]
Genetic Variance-Covariance Structure:
a ~ N(0, G ⊗ A) where G = [σ²ₐTNB σₐTNB,NBA σₐTNB,LW ] [σₐNBA,TNB σ²ₐNBA σₐNBA,LW ] [σₐLW,TNB σₐLW,NBA σ²ₐLW ]
Weighting Matrix:
Var(e) = W × diag(σ²ₑTNB, σ²ₑNBA, σ²ₑLW) ⊗ I

where W = weighting factor based on number of observations per sow

Breeding Simulation Analysis
SelAction 2.1 Framework
Population Structure:
  • 1:5 mating ratio (200 boars, 1,000 sows)
  • 6 male + 6 female piglets kept per sow
  • Selection intensity: 0.033 (males), 0.167 (females)
Information Sources:
  • Sows: Own performance + BLUP + 5 full-sib + 24 half-sib
  • Boars: BLUP + 6 full-sib + 24 half-sib information
Input Parameters:

Heritabilities, phenotypic variations, and genetic/phenotypic correlations from ASReml results

Four Breeding Scenarios
Scenario 1: Mean Increase Only
  • Economic value = 1 for single mean trait
  • Result: 1.1-2.8% yearly increase in variability
Scenario 2: Variability Decrease Only
  • Economic value = -1 for single LnVar trait
  • Result: Similar magnitude decrease in means
Scenario 3: Maintain Mean + Reduce Variability
  • Mean: economic value ≈ 0.11, LnVar: -1
  • Result: Difficult to achieve, limited success
Scenario 4: Increase Mean + Reduce Variability
  • Mean: economic value ≈ 0.11, LnVar: -1
  • Result: Only NBA scenario somewhat successful

Practical Implications & Findings
Maximum Traits: Use with Caution
  • Higher heritability: 2.5× higher than classical traits
  • Faster genetic gain: Theoretical advantage for selection
  • Hidden costs: Increased stillborn ratio and trait variability
  • Recommendation: Use for identification of early-maturing sows, not direct selection
  • Optimal use: Identify sows reaching peak performance by parity 3
Sow Longevity Insights
  • Extended productivity: 15% of sows reach maximum after parity 6
  • Economic impact: Retaining productive sows beyond parity 5 beneficial
  • Selection timing: Maintain selection decisions at parity 3
  • Management strategy: Use genomic selection to predict late-parity performance
  • Industry practice: Challenge current culling strategies
Complex Correlation Challenge: The strong positive genetic correlations (0.66-0.74) between mean and variability traits create a fundamental trade-off - improving average performance automatically increases variation, making simultaneous improvement in both directions extremely challenging.
Balanced Breeding Solution: Implement multi-trait selection indices that explicitly include variability traits with appropriate economic weights, focusing on sustainable genetic gain while maintaining trait uniformity for improved animal welfare and farm management.
Future Directions & Limitations
Research Limitations
  • Single breed focus: Results specific to Large White pigs
  • Minimum record requirement: ≥5 parities may bias toward longer-lived sows
  • Limited genomic information: No molecular genetic analysis
  • Simulation constraints: Theoretical breeding scenarios vs. real-world complexity
Industry Impact & Future Research
Immediate Applications:
  • Breeding program design incorporating variability traits
  • Economic weight determination for balanced selection
  • Sow management strategies for extended productive life
  • Genomic selection for predicting maximum performance timing
Future Research Needs:
  • Genomic analysis of variability traits
  • Multi-breed validation studies
  • Integration with functional trait selection (teat number, milk production)
  • Economic modeling of welfare costs vs. production benefits
  • Investigation of epigenetic effects on trait variability
