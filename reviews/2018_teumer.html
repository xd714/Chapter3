Research Context & Causal Inference Challenge
Fundamental Problem: Observational epidemiological studies struggle with causal inference due to unobserved confounding, reverse causation, and selection bias. Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) address some issues but face ethical constraints, long duration, and compliance problems.

Citation: Teumer, A. (2018). Common Methods for Performing Mendelian Randomization. Frontiers in Cardiovascular Medicine, 5, 51.

Core Methodological Foundation:

  • Mendelian laws: Alleles segregate randomly from parents to offspring
  • Genetic variants as instrumental variables for exposures
  • GWAS discoveries: >950 cardiovascular-related associations by April 2018

Mendelian Randomization Framework
1
Core MR Principle
Use genetic variants (Z) as instrumental variables for exposure (X) to estimate causal effect on outcome (Y)
Key Analogy: Random inheritance ‚âà randomized treatment assignment
Path: Z ‚Üí X ‚Üí Y (mediated effect)
Advantage: Unconfounded exposure-outcome relationship
Applications: Cardiovascular diseases, nephrology, metabolic traits
2
Three Core Assumptions
Essential requirements for valid Mendelian randomization
Assumption 1: Genetic variant associated with exposure (testable: F-statistic >10)
Assumption 2: Exclusion restriction - IV affects outcome only through exposure
Assumption 3: IV independent of confounders of exposure-outcome relationship
Challenge: Assumptions 2 & 3 difficult to validate (pleiotropy, linkage disequilibrium)
3
Statistical Methods Overview
Multiple approaches for causal effect estimation
2-Stage Least Squares (2SLS): Single sample, implicit two-step process
Ratio Estimator (Wald): Triangulation approach, allows two-sample MR
Control Function: Two-step approach for dichotomous outcomes
Multiple IV methods: Allele scores, inverse variance weighting, MR-Egger
4
GWAS Integration
Leverage large-scale genetic association studies
Data Source: >50,000 SNP-trait associations from GWAS Catalog
Two-sample MR: Use separate studies for exposure and outcome
Power Enhancement: Large sample sizes from meta-analyses
Accessibility: Publicly available association results enable powerful studies

Approach: Two-step method for dichotomous outcomes
Step 1: X ~ Z, save residuals
Step 2: Y ~ X + residuals
Mechanism:
  • Residuals capture non-instrumented exposure
  • Control for unobserved confounders
  • Equivalent to 2SLS for continuous outcomes
  • Suitable for logistic regression models
Advanced Methods & Bias Considerations
WHY - Need for MR
Observational Study Limitations: Traditional epidemiology faces fundamental challenges in establishing causality.
  • Unobserved confounding biases exposure-outcome associations
  • Reverse causation: disease may affect exposure levels
  • Selection bias in study populations
  • RCTs limited by ethics, duration, and compliance issues
MR Solution: Genetic variants provide natural randomization analogous to RCTs.
HOW - Multiple Instruments
Addressing Weak Instrument Bias: Single SNPs often explain <1% of phenotypic variance.
  • Allele Scores: Weighted/unweighted sum of risk alleles
  • Inverse Variance Weighting: Meta-analysis approach for combining estimates
  • MR-Egger Regression: Relaxes exclusion restriction under InSIDE condition
  • Power Enhancement: Multiple instruments increase explained variance
Implementation: R packages (gtx, metafor, TwoSampleMR), MRbase platform.
WHAT - Bias Sources
Assumption Violations: Different bias patterns depending on which assumptions fail.
  • Weak Instruments: Bias toward observational association
  • Pleiotropy: SNPs affect outcome via multiple pathways
  • Linkage Disequilibrium: Tag SNPs capture effects of nearby genes
  • Population Stratification: Confounding by ancestry
Bias Formula: Œ≤ÃÇXY = Œ≤causal + Œ±ZY/Œ≤ZX (for exclusion restriction violation).

Pleiotropy Assessment
Detection Methods:
  • MR-Egger intercept test for directional pleiotropy
  • Heterogeneity tests across multiple instruments
  • Biological pathway analysis of candidate genes
  • Sensitivity analyses excluding potentially pleiotropic SNPs
InSIDE Condition Evaluation:
  • Assess correlation between instrument strength and direct effects
  • Compare results across different MR methods
  • Use prior biological knowledge about gene functions
Future Developments:

  • Machine learning approaches for invalid instrument identification

Limitations & Future Challenges
Current Limitations
  • Linear Effects Assumption: May not capture complex dose-response relationships
  • Population Ancestry: Most GWAS based on European populations
  • Effect Modification: Difficult to assess interactions with other variables
  • Rare Variants: Limited power for low-frequency genetic variants
Statistical Challenges
  • Weak Instruments: Small effect sizes require very large sample sizes
  • Pleiotropy: Difficult to distinguish from true causal effects
  • Binary Outcomes: Rare disease assumption often violated
  • Missing Heritability: Known variants explain small proportion of trait variance

Method Selection Guidelines
Single Sample MR
When to Use:
  • Complete individual-level data available
  • Need to adjust for additional covariates
  • Subgroup analyses required
  • Complex models with interactions
Preferred Methods:
  • 2SLS for continuous outcomes
  • Control function for binary outcomes
  • Multiple instruments via allele scores
Two Sample MR
When to Use:
  • Large GWAS summary statistics available
  • Different optimal samples for exposure/outcome
  • Enhanced statistical power needed
  • No individual-level data access
Preferred Methods:
  • Ratio estimator for single instruments
  • Inverse variance weighting for multiple instruments
  • MR-Egger for pleiotropy assessment
