Comparing Deregression Methods for Genomic Prediction of Test-Day Traits in Dairy Cattle

Optimizing Pseudophenotypes for Longitudinal Genomic Selection

Authors: H.R. de Oliveira, F.F. Silva, L.F. Brito, A.R. Guarini, J. Jamrozik, F.S. Schenkel

Institutions: University of Guelph, Universidade Federal de Viçosa, Canadian Dairy Network

Year: 2018

View Original Paper

Paper Overview & Research Context

Core Challenge: In dairy cattle genomic evaluation, when raw phenotypic data aren't available, estimated breeding values (EBVs) must be used as pseudophenotypes. However, using EBVs directly can cause double-counting of information and bias in genomic predictions.

Research Question: Which deregression method performs best for converting EBVs to unbiased pseudophenotypes in genomic prediction of test-day dairy traits?

Innovation: First comprehensive comparison of deregression methods specifically for longitudinal (test-day) traits in dairy cattle, examining three scenarios based on reliability thresholds.

Deregression Methods Comparison
VanRaden (VR) Method
Approach: Standard deregression formula
dEBVij = PAij + (EBVij - PAij) / Ri
Key Features:
• Uses reliability-based deregression factor
• Separates parent average from own performance
• Most widely used method

Performance:
• Good reliability and low bias
• Stable across scenarios
• No weighting required
Wiggans (WG) Method
Approach: VR method + cow adjustment
Adjust cow dEBVs to match bull distribution
Key Features:
• Variance adjustment (VA) by reliability groups
• Mean adjustment (MA) for each group
• Addresses cow-bull bias differences

Performance:
• Similar reliability to VR
• Reduces cow-bull differences
• Effective for preferential treatment bias
Garrick (GR) Method
Approach: Mixed model equations approximation
Solves system: W'PA WPA + λk vs W'IndPA WIndPA + 2k
Key Features:
• More complex mathematical formulation
• Accounts for information content directly
• Theoretically sophisticated

Performance:
• Lower reliability than VR/WG
• Too strong deregression
• Higher variance in dEBVs
Experimental Design & Methodology
1
Data Source & Population Structure
Canadian Jersey cattle with official genetic evaluations for test-day traits
Training Population: Animals born 1960-2010 (from 2012 evaluation)
Validation Population: Animals born 2011-2014 (from 2016 evaluation)
Traits: Milk yield (MY), fat yield (FY), protein yield (PY), somatic cell score (SCS)
Random Regression: 4th-order Legendre polynomials for lactation curves
2
Reliability-Based Scenarios
Three scenarios based on minimum reliability thresholds (RELEBV)
SCEN1: RELEBV > average parent reliability in both populations
SCEN2: RELEBV > 0.50 in training, > average parent reliability in validation
SCEN3: RELEBV > 0.50 in both training and validation
Purpose: Test effect of reliability thresholds on prediction accuracy
3
Genomic Prediction Implementation
GBLUP with random regression model using deregressed EBVs as pseudophenotypes
Model: y = Xb + Zg + e (4th-order Legendre polynomials)
Weighting: Unweighted vs. weighted by effective record contributions
Genotypes: 38,166 SNPs after quality control
Software: AIREMLF90 for variance components and solutions
4
Evaluation Metrics
Comprehensive assessment of prediction quality and bias
Reliability: r²dEBV, r²DGV, adjusted r²DGV
Bias: Regression coefficient of dEBV on DGV (bDGV)
Separate Analysis: Bulls vs. cows performance
Total Analyses: 18 per trait (3 methods × 3 scenarios × 2 weighting)
Key Findings & Results
Method Performance Ranking
Best Performers:
• VR and WG methods: Similar reliability and bias
• SCEN2: Optimal balance of accuracy and population size
• Unweighted analysis sufficient in most cases

Poor Performer:
• GR method: Lower reliability across all scenarios
• Too strong deregression causing information loss
• Higher variance in deregressed values
Practical Implications
Reliability Thresholds:
• SCEN3 (0.50 minimum): Highest raw reliability
• SCEN2: Best after adjusting for dEBV inaccuracy
• Balance between accuracy and population size

Weighting Benefits:
• Only helpful when large RELEBV differences exist
• Most beneficial for protein yield and SCS
• Generally unnecessary for VR/WG methods
Why, How, What Analysis
WHY - Biological & Statistical Challenge
Deregression Necessity:
• EBVs contain parent average information
• Using raw EBVs causes double-counting
• Double shrinkage reduces prediction accuracy

Test-Day Complexity:
• Longitudinal traits require special handling
• Lactation curve shape must be preserved
• Bulls vs. cows have different evaluation bias
HOW - Methodological Approach
Deregression Process:
• Remove parent average contribution from EBV
• Weight by reliability to avoid over-correction
• WG method adjusts cow bias

Validation Strategy:
• Time-based split (2012 vs. 2016 evaluations)
• Multiple reliability thresholds tested
• Separate bull/cow analysis reveals bias patterns
WHAT - Practical Recommendations
Best Practice:
• Use VR or WG methods
• SCEN2 reliability threshold optimal
• Unweighted analysis generally sufficient

Implementation:
• Preserve lactation curve coefficients
• Consider cow preferential treatment
• Monitor bull vs. cow prediction bias
Technical Implementation Details
A
Cow Bias Adjustment (WG Method)
Addresses preferential treatment of genotyped cows in Jersey population:
PAadj = PAsire + [VA × (PAdam - MA)]
dEBVadj,cows = VA × (dEBV - MA) + PAadj
This adjustment makes cow evaluations comparable to bull evaluations.
B
Reliability-Based Population Selection
Strategic thresholds balance accuracy and population size:
Average RELPA: 0.35 (MY), 0.35 (FY), 0.34 (PY), 0.31 (SCS)
Population Impact: SCEN2/3 had 20-55% fewer animals than SCEN1
Cow Proportion: ~40% except SCS (17% in SCEN2/3)
Implications for Dairy Breeding
Implications for Dairy Breeding

Immediate Impact: Provides clear guidance for implementing genomic prediction in dairy breeding programs using test-day evaluations, particularly important for breeds with smaller populations that include cow records.

Methodological Significance: Demonstrates that established deregression methods developed for accumulated traits work well for longitudinal traits, with VR and WG methods providing nearly equivalent performance.

Future Applications: The finding that weighting is generally unnecessary simplifies implementation in breeding programs, while the cow adjustment method (WG) provides a solution for addressing preferential treatment bias in female evaluations.
