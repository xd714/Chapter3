Use of Haplotypes to Estimate Mendelian Sampling Effects and Selection Limits

Genomic Breeding Strategy and Selection Theory

Authors: J.B. Cole, P.M. VanRaden

Institution: Animal Improvement Programs Laboratory, ARS, USDA, Beltsville, MD, USA

Journal: Journal of Animal Breeding and Genetics, 128 (2011) 446–455

View Original Paper

Paper Information & Context

Citation: Cole, J.B., & VanRaden, P.M. (2011). Use of haplotypes to estimate Mendelian sampling effects and selection limits. Journal of Animal Breeding and Genetics, 128(6), 446-455.

Research Context: This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in genomic selection: predicting the unpredictable Mendelian sampling component of genetic merit using haplotype information from dense SNP genotyping.

Key Innovation

First comprehensive framework for using haplotypes to estimate Mendelian sampling variance and calculate theoretical selection limits in dairy cattle populations using dense SNP marker data.

Why, How, What Analysis
WHY - Problem Statement
Core Challenge: Mendelian sampling (MS) variance creates unpredictable genetic variation within families that traditional breeding values cannot capture.
  • Traditional limitation: Parent average (PA) doesn't predict individual MS effects
  • Breeding impact: Genomic selection's increased reliability comes from better MS estimation
  • Population risk: Intensive selection reduces genetic diversity while increasing inbreeding
  • Opportunity: Dense SNP data enables direct inspection of chromosomal-level genetic variation
Research Gap: No methodology existed to systematically estimate MS variance bounds and theoretical selection limits using haplotype information.
HOW - Methodology
Haplotype-Based MS Estimation: Two-boundary approach using linkage assumptions.
  • Lower bound (MSU): Assumes all loci unlinked - individual marker effects
  • Upper bound (MSC): Assumes perfect linkage within chromosomes
  • Validation: Expected MS (½Va) falls between bounds
  • Selection limits: Combine best haplotypes vs. best individual alleles
Datasets: 43,382 SNPs in Holstein (40,351), Brown Swiss (1,455), and Jersey (4,064) cattle using BovineSNP50 BeadChip.
WHAT - Key Findings
MS Variance Patterns: Clear breed differences in genetic potential.
  • Holstein superiority: Largest MS variance and selection limits across all traits
  • Selection potential: Current best animals well below theoretical limits
  • Inbreeding relationship: Strong negative correlation (-0.73 to -0.86) between genomic inbreeding and MSU
  • Practical implications: Haplotype selection could dramatically improve genetic merit
Selection Limits: Holstein NM$ limits of $7,515 (lower) to $23,588 (upper) vs. current best $2,528.
Mathematical Framework
Mendelian Sampling Variance (Complete Linkage)
MSC = Σc=130 (Σm=1nc smam - Σm=1nc dmam)2
Upper bound assuming perfect linkage within chromosomes
Mendelian Sampling Variance (No Linkage)
MSU = Σm=143,382 (smam - dmam)2
Lower bound assuming all loci unlinked
Selection Limits
SLC = Σc=130 maxH (Σm=1nc hmam)
SLU = Σm=143,382 maxL (lmam)
Where H = haplotype set, L = loci set, am = marker effect
Methodology Flow
1
Genotype Data Processing
Obtain dense SNP genotypes using BovineSNP50 BeadChip
SNP markers: 43,382 across genome
Populations: Holstein (40,351), Brown Swiss (1,455), Jersey (4,064)
Quality control: Standard genomic evaluation pipeline
Marker effects: Estimated using Bayes A model (h=1.12)
2
Haplotype Imputation
Reconstruct parental chromosome combinations using findhap.f90
Method: Combined population and pedigree haplotyping
Program: Fortran findhap.f90 (VanRaden et al. 2011)
Output: Maternal and paternal haplotypes per chromosome
Validation: Comparison with known parent-offspring relationships
3
MS Variance Calculation
Compute upper and lower bounds using linkage assumptions
MSC calculation: Perfect linkage within chromosomes
MSU calculation: All markers unlinked
Expected MS: ½ additive genetic variance (validation)
Traits analyzed: DPR, milk yield, NM$, protein yield
4
Selection Limit Estimation
Calculate theoretical genetic merit limits using optimal haplotype combinations
Lower bound (SLC): Best 30 haplotypes (one per chromosome)
Upper bound (SLU): Best allele at each locus independently
Adjustment: Inbreeding correction using homozygosity effects
Comparison: Current population vs. theoretical limits
5
Correlation Analysis
Examine relationships between inbreeding, MS variance, and genetic merit
Genomic inbreeding: Marker-based homozygosity calculation
MS correlations: Validation of theoretical expectations
Temporal trends: 1990-2010 genetic variance changes
Merit relationships: GEBV correlation with MS variance
Key Results by Trait
Lifetime Net Merit (NM$)
Holstein Performance:
  • Lower selection limit: $7,515
  • Upper selection limit: $23,588
  • Current best DGV: $2,528
  • Best active bull GEBV: $1,588
Genetic Potential: ~5× improvement possible with optimal haplotype selection
Daughter Pregnancy Rate (%)
Fertility Improvement Potential:
  • Holstein limits: 40% (lower) to 139% (upper)
  • Current best: 8% across breeds
  • Strong genetic basis for fertility improvement
Breeding Impact: Substantial fertility gains possible despite low heritability
Milk & Protein Yield
Production Limits:
  • Milk: 11,310 kg (lower) to 35,419 kg (upper) in Holstein
  • Protein: 312 kg (lower) to 1,138 kg (upper) in Holstein
  • Current levels well below theoretical maximums
Selection History: Despite 40+ years of milk selection, substantial genetic potential remains
Inbreeding and MS Variance Relationships
Genomic Inbreeding Effects
Strong Negative Correlations with MSU:
  • Brown Swiss: -0.73 to -0.86
  • Holstein: -0.12 to -0.84
  • Jersey: -0.01 to -0.86
Validation: Confirms theoretical expectation that inbreeding reduces MS variance
Temporal Trends (1990-2010)
MS Variance Changes:
  • Slight negative slopes across breeds
  • Only Holstein significant (p < 0.05)
  • Inbreeding increased but MS variance maintained
Interpretation: Elite animals not heavily inbred; genetic diversity preserved in selection candidates
Practical Applications
Breeding Program Applications
Mate Selection Strategies:
  • Factorial mating designs using haplotype information
  • Optimization of MS variance in offspring
  • Balance between genetic gain and diversity maintenance
  • Embryo screening potential for extreme genetic merit
Implementation: 2^29 possible autosome combinations provide vast selection potential
Population Management
Genetic Diversity Strategies:
  • Optimal contribution schemes balancing gain and inbreeding
  • MS variance as selection criterion
  • Long-term genetic sustainability
  • Haplotype diversity preservation
Future Directions: Integration with genomic selection for sustainable genetic improvement
Study Limitations and Future Directions
Current Limitations
Methodological Constraints:
  • Marker effect shrinkage: Bayesian regression underestimates true genetic variance
  • Linkage assumptions: Perfect vs. no linkage may not reflect biological reality
  • Population assumptions: Closed populations without mutation or epistasis
  • Time horizon: Long-term selection may change allele frequencies substantially
Future Research Opportunities
Methodological Advances:
  • Incorporate recombination mapping and LD block structure
  • Develop dynamic selection limit models accounting for allele frequency changes
  • Integrate epistatic effects and QTL interactions
  • Extend to other livestock species and traits
Practical Applications:
  • Commercial breeding program implementation
  • Economic optimization of genetic gain vs. diversity trade-offs
  • Integration with genomic prediction software
Impact and Significance

Scientific Contribution: This paper established the theoretical foundation for haplotype-based Mendelian sampling estimation, providing the first systematic approach to calculate selection limits using dense genomic data. The methodology has influenced subsequent research in genomic selection optimization and genetic diversity management.

Practical Impact: The framework enables breeding programs to:

  • Quantify theoretical genetic potential in livestock populations
  • Optimize mating decisions using haplotype information
  • Balance short-term genetic gain with long-term sustainability
  • Implement advanced selection strategies beyond traditional genomic prediction

Long-term Influence: The concepts introduced have been extended to optimal contribution selection, mate allocation algorithms, and modern breeding program design, representing a fundamental advance in quantitative genetics methodology.
