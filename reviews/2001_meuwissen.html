Prediction of Total Genetic Value Using Genome-Wide Dense Marker Maps

The Foundational Paper of Genomic Selection

Authors: T. H. E. Meuwissen, B. J. Hayes, M. E. Goddard

Journal: Genetics, Vol. 157, pp. 1819-1829, 2001

Institution: Research Institute of Animal Science and Health, Victorian Institute of Animal Science, University of Melbourne

View Original Paper

Revolutionary Concept & Problem Statement
üéØ Paradigm Shift

First proposal to use dense genome-wide marker maps (~50,000 markers) to predict total genetic value without establishing linkage phase in each family - a fundamental departure from traditional QTL mapping approaches that launched the genomic selection era.

Core Challenge: Traditional marker-assisted selection was limited because linkage phase between markers and QTL had to be established separately for every family. Dense marker maps offered the potential to capture all genetic variance through linkage disequilibrium, but created a "large p, small n" problem - estimating ~50,000 effects from only 2,200 phenotypic records.

Citation: Meuwissen, T. H. E., Hayes, B. J., & Goddard, M. E. (2001). Prediction of total genetic value using genome-wide dense marker maps. Genetics, 157(4), 1819-1829.

Historical Context:

  • Published before human genome completion and SNP chip technology
  • Predicted that SNP density and genotyping would become cost-effective
  • Introduced concept of using linkage disequilibrium across populations
  • Laid theoretical foundation for what became multi-billion dollar genomic selection industry

Comprehensive Results Analysis
WHY - Genomic Selection Superiority
Revolutionary Advantage: Genomic selection achieved accuracy comparable to progeny testing without requiring performance records.
  • BayesB accuracy: 0.848 vs pedigree-based: ~0.4
  • No need to establish linkage phase per family
  • Captures effects across entire genome simultaneously
  • Utilizes population-wide linkage disequilibrium
  • Enables selection of juveniles without phenotypes
Impact: Transforms breeding from family-based to population-based genomic prediction.
HOW - Technical Breakthrough
Core Innovation: Overcame "large p, small n" problem through sophisticated Bayesian approaches.
  • Haplotype Construction: Combined flanking markers into ~50,000 effects
  • Prior Knowledge: Used realistic distributions of QTL effects
  • Variable Selection: BayesB automatically identified important regions
  • Computational Methods: MCMC sampling with efficient algorithms
  • Cross-Validation: Rigorous evaluation on independent generation
Key Insight: Most genomic regions have zero effect; few have large effects.
WHAT - Performance Validation
Quantitative Results: Demonstrated clear superiority of Bayesian methods over traditional approaches.
  • LS Accuracy: 0.318 (severely biased, b = 0.285)
  • BLUP Accuracy: 0.732 (good but limited assumptions)
  • BayesA Accuracy: 0.798 (improvement over BLUP)
  • BayesB Accuracy: 0.848 (best overall performance)
Practical Impact: Established genomic selection as viable alternative to traditional breeding.
