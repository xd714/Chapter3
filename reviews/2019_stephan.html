Selective Sweeps: Theory, Detection, and Evolutionary Implications

Comprehensive Review of Population Genetic Models and Statistical Methods

Author: Wolfgang Stephan

Institution: Leibniz-Institute for Evolution and Biodiversity Science, Berlin, Germany

🔗 Original Paper (Genetics 2019)

Theoretical Foundation & Historical Context

Citation: Stephan, W. (2019). Selective Sweeps. Genetics, 211(1), 5-13.

Historical Development: The concept of genetic hitchhiking was first introduced by Maynard Smith and Haigh (1974) in their seminal paper "The hitch-hiking effect of a favorable gene," building on earlier observations of periodic selection in bacteria by Atwood et al. (1951).

Core Principle: When a strongly advantageous mutation spreads through a population by directional selection, linked neutral variants inevitably increase in frequency, potentially reducing genetic variation in the genomic region surrounding the selected site.

Theoretical Models of Selective Sweeps
1
Basic Hitchhiking Model
Foundation model describing how beneficial alleles affect linked neutral variation
Key Parameter: c/s ratio (recombination rate/selection coefficient)
Effect: Determines size of region with reduced variation
Prediction: Genetic variation eliminated at selected site, preserved at distant loci
2
Stochastic Extensions
Incorporation of genetic drift and finite population effects
Methods: Coalescent theory (Kaplan et al. 1989), Diffusion equations (Stephan et al. 1992)
Requirement: Strong selection (Nes >> 1)
Agreement: Excellent match with deterministic predictions
3
Recurrent Sweeps Model
Multiple sequential sweeps occurring across the genome
Formula: π = π₀ × r/(r + κan)
Parameters: n = sweep rate, a = 2Nes intensity
Application: Species with large effective population sizes
4
Competing Sweeps (Evolutionary Traffic)
Simultaneous sweeps at multiple loci with interference effects
Effect: Reduced fixation probability of beneficial alleles
Impact: Weakened hitchhiking effect
Significance: Important in low recombination regions
Hard vs. Soft Sweeps
Hard Sweeps
Origin: New mutation or very rare allele

Characteristics:
• Single haplotype carries beneficial allele
• Complete elimination of variation at selected site
• Classic hitchhiking pattern
• Strong linkage disequilibrium signature

Signatures:
• Reduced nucleotide diversity
• Excess of low-frequency variants
• Elevated linkage disequilibrium
• Characteristic site frequency spectrum
Soft Sweeps
Origin: Standing genetic variation

Characteristics:
• Multiple haplotypes carry beneficial allele
• Partial preservation of variation
• Arises from environmental change
• Less pronounced signatures

Examples:
• Lactase persistence in humans
• Eda locus in sticklebacks
• Local adaptation scenarios
• Response to selective pressure changes
Statistical Detection Methods
CLR Test
Composite Likelihood Ratio test detecting local reductions in nucleotide diversity. Compares neutral model vs. selective sweep model.
SweepFinder
Genome-wide CLR-based method using empirical background distribution. More robust against some demographic scenarios.
SweeD
Computationally advanced CLR test with demographic modeling. Includes arbitrary population size changes.
iHS Test
Haplotype-based test detecting ongoing sweeps. Exploits long haplotypes under strong linkage disequilibrium.
GOF Test
Goodness-of-fit approach used with CLR to distinguish true sweeps from demographic effects like bottlenecks.
XP-CLR
Extended CLR for substructured populations. Detects differentiation patterns caused by local adaptation.
Key Signatures and Molecular Patterns
📊
Site Frequency Spectrum (SFS) Patterns
Single Sweeps: Excess of low-frequency and high-frequency derived alleles
Recurrent Sweeps: Excess of high-frequency variants disappears
Soft Sweeps: Less pronounced distortions in frequency spectrum
🧬
Linkage Disequilibrium Signatures
During Fixation: Elevated LD levels around selected site
After Fixation: Complete breakdown of LD across selected site
Haplotype Structure: Long haplotypes indicate ongoing selection
Confounding Factors and Challenges
Demographic Confounding
Population Bottlenecks: Can mimic sweep signatures with up to 80% false positive rates
  • Population size changes affect genetic diversity
  • Recent severe bottlenecks particularly problematic
  • Combined CLR+GOF tests help distinguish
  • Demographic modeling increasingly important
Solutions: Joint inference of demographic and selective history
Background Selection
Competing Model: Continuous removal of deleterious mutations also reduces variation
  • Similar patterns to selective sweeps
  • Affects regions of low recombination
  • Proposed as null hypothesis for scan methods
  • Joint modeling approaches developed
Distinguishing Features: Pattern differs in recombination correlation
Population Structure
Subdivided Populations: Affect sweep dynamics and detection
  • Slower fixation in subdivided populations
  • Incomplete sweeps more common
  • Migration patterns influence signatures
  • FST-based methods for local adaptation
Applications: Human populations show extensive incomplete sweeps
Applications and Empirical Findings
🔬
Genome-Wide Scans
Early genome scans in humans, Drosophila melanogaster, and mice revealed surprisingly large numbers of putative sweep signatures, highlighting the need for demographic controls and the prevalence of linked selection across genomes.
📈
Evolutionary Rate Estimation
Sweep models enable estimation of adaptive substitution rates and selection strength. The frequency of beneficial substitutions can be accurately estimated even when individual targets are difficult to localize.
Future Directions and Outstanding Questions
Theoretical Challenges:
  • Evolutionary Traffic: Better predictions needed for interfering selective sweeps
  • Joint Modeling: Simultaneous inference of demography and weak selection
  • Population Structure: Mathematical treatment of subdivided populations remains difficult
Experimental Validation:
  • Search for causative nucleotide changes has only recently begun
  • Manipulative experiments needed to test hypotheses generated by scan methods
  • Fine-mapping approaches showing promise for target identification
