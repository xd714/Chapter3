üéØ Study Overview and Objectives
üî¨ Research Question

This study aims to characterize and compare protein-coding variation between two distinct outbred laboratory mouse stocks - J:ARC (Swiss-derived) and J:DO (multiparent) - to provide molecular basis for their intended research applications and guide experimental design decisions.

The Challenge: Different outbred mouse stocks have varying genetic architectures, but researchers often lack molecular data to guide stock selection for specific study designs. Understanding these differences is crucial for reproducible research and optimal experimental outcomes.
1
Study Populations
J:ARC (Swiss-derived):
  • 64 mice (32 male, 32 female) from generation G3
  • Imported from Australian Animal Resource Center in 2020
  • Traces origin to Swiss mice from Rockefeller Institute (1926)
  • Maintained using Poiley rotational breeding scheme
J:DO (Diversity Outbred):
  • 20 mice (10 male, 10 female) from generation G43
  • Derived from 8 founder inbred strains
  • Represents three major mouse subspecies
  • Captures 90% of genetic variation in laboratory mice

üìà Statistical Analysis Approach
Heterozygosity Calculations
Individual Heterozygosity:
Het_rate = (N(NM) - O(Hom)) / N(NM)
Where:
  • N(NM): Number of non-missing genotypes
  • O(Hom): Observed homozygous genotypes
  • Calculated using Plink --het option
Per-Variant Heterozygosity:
  • Number of heterozygous samples / total genotyped samples
  • Used for variant-level diversity comparison
Population Structure Analysis
Principal Component Analysis:
  • Joint variant calling on both populations
  • 120,862 shared variants used for comparison
  • PC1 captures interstrain variation
  • PC2 reveals interindividual differences
Linkage Disequilibrium:
  • Pairwise r¬≤ calculation for autosomal SNPs
  • 95th percentile values for SNPs up to 1Mb apart
  • Monomorphic and high-missing variants removed
Sex Difference Testing
XtX Statistic Calculation:
  • Locus-specific F_ST corrected for covariance
  • Estimated using BayPass software
  • Tests for male vs. female allele frequency differences
  • Benjamini-Hochberg correction applied (FDR = 0.05)
MCMC Parameters:
  • 20 pilot runs of 500 iterations
  • Burn-in: 5,000 iterations
  • Sample: 1,000 iterations (thinning = 20)
  • Three independent runs for convergence

üåü Novel Variant Insights

The study revealed that J:DO mice carry nearly twice as many high-impact protein-damaging variants compared to J:ARC, yet maintain normal fecundity. This highlights the buffering effects of high allelic diversity and heterozygosity, where deleterious effects can be masked by functional alleles at the same or compensatory loci.

Sample Size Recommendations
For Genetic Mapping Studies: J:DO populations require fewer animals to capture equivalent genetic diversity compared to J:ARC. However, the higher interindividual variation in J:DO may require larger sample sizes to detect specific effect sizes, depending on the trait architecture and study objectives.

Poiley Rotational Breeding System

  • Systematic rotation to minimize inbreeding

  • G0: Imported founders from ARC Australia

Diversity Optimization:
  • 175 breeding funnels for maximum diversity
  • MateSel software for optimal breeding decisions
  • Minimizes inadvertent phenotypic selection
  • Prevents population structure formation
Quality Control Measures:
  • Pedigreed population history tracking
  • Random breeder assignment (blinded)
  • Ongoing genetic monitoring
  • Periodic removal of deleterious alleles (e.g., R2D2)

Breeding System Impact on Genetic Architecture:

  • J:ARC's smaller funnel system creates more structured population with siblings
  • J:DO's large funnel system with MateSel maintains random mating
  • Different selective pressures: fecundity (J:ARC) vs. diversity (J:DO)
  • Results in distinct patterns of allele frequency and heterozygosity
üí≠ Discussion and Interpretation
üîç Key Biological Insights

The study reveals fundamental differences between single-origin and multi-parent outbred populations. The tolerance of J:DO mice to high loads of protein-damaging variants demonstrates the protective effects of genetic diversity, while J:ARC's optimized characteristics reflect decades of selection for laboratory traits.

Evolutionary Perspectives
Selection Pressures:
  • J:ARC: Artificial selection for fecundity and laboratory traits
  • J:DO: Maintained diversity with minimal artificial selection
  • Different trajectories of deleterious allele removal
  • Contrasting genetic load tolerance mechanisms
Population Genetics Principles:
  • Genetic drift effects in smaller effective populations
  • Balancing selection maintaining diversity in J:DO
  • Founder effects from Swiss origin bottleneck
Functional Genomics Implications
Gene Function Categories:
  • J:DO novel variants: Enriched in post-translational modifications
  • DNA repair pathway genes affected
  • Translational regulation and RNA binding
  • Higher functional impact tolerance
Compensatory Mechanisms:
  • Allelic buffering in heterozygous state
  • Functional redundancy across gene families
  • Regulatory compensation for dosage effects
Translational Relevance
Human Disease Modeling:
  • J:DO better represents human genetic diversity
  • J:ARC provides standardized disease models
  • Different utility for rare vs. common diseases
  • Pharmacogenomics applications vary by stock
Clinical Translation:

üéØ Study Impact and Future Applications
Scientific Contribution
This study provides the first comprehensive molecular comparison of J:ARC and J:DO outbred mouse stocks, establishing evidence-based guidelines for stock selection in biomedical research. The findings demonstrate how different breeding strategies and genetic origins create distinct research tools optimized for different applications.
Evidence-Based Recommendations:
  • Choose J:ARC for: Toxicology, pharmacology, general research requiring reproducibility
  • Choose J:DO for: Genetic mapping, complex trait studies, translational research
  • Sample Size Planning: Fewer J:ARC needed for trait coverage, more J:DO for population diversity
  • Power Calculations: Consider genetic architecture differences in study design
  • Quality Control: Regular genetic monitoring essential for both stocks
Research Gaps and Future Priorities
Immediate Needs: Whole genome sequencing for complete variant catalogs, long-read sequencing for structural variants, multi-tissue expression profiling, and longitudinal genetic stability studies.

Long-term Goals: Development of additional outbred populations with intermediate characteristics, integration with phenotypic databases, and establishment of genetic quality control standards for the research community.
Lasting Impact: This work establishes a framework for molecular characterization of outbred laboratory animal populations, providing tools for evidence-based stock selection and quality control that will enhance reproducibility and optimize research outcomes across biomedical research applications.
