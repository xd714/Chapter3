Genome-wide association mapping including phenotypes from relatives without genotypes

Bridging Genomic Selection and GWAS Through Single-Step Methodology

Authors: H. Wang, I. Misztal, I. Aguilar, A. Legarra, W.M. Muir

Journal: Genetics Research Cambridge, Vol. 94, pp. 73-83, 2012

Institution: University of Georgia, INIA Las Brujas, INRA, Purdue University

🎯 The Core Innovation: Single-Step GWAS
🔬 Breaking the Genotyping Barrier

Traditional GWAS requires all animals to be genotyped, severely limiting sample sizes and statistical power. This paper introduces single-step GBLUP (ssGBLUP) for GWAS, which can utilize phenotypic information from thousands of ungenotyped relatives to dramatically improve QTL detection power and precision without increasing genotyping costs.

The Challenge: Classical GWAS and Bayesian methods (BayesA/BayesB) could only use phenotypes from genotyped animals. This created a fundamental limitation: either genotype everyone (expensive) or accept reduced power. Multi-step approaches using pseudo-data were complex and often suboptimal.
The Innovation: ssGBLUP seamlessly blends pedigree and genomic relationships in a single analysis, allowing direct use of phenotypes from ungenotyped relatives. This paper extends ssGBLUP to derive SNP effects and weights for GWAS applications.
📐 Mathematical Framework
Core ssGBLUP Model
y = 1μ + Zaa + e
Where y = phenotypes, a = animal effects, H blends pedigree and genomic relationships
Unified Relationship Matrix H
The Key Innovation:
H-1 = A-1 + [0 0; 0 G-1 - A22-1]
Components:
  • A: Pedigree relationship matrix (all animals)
  • A22: Pedigree relationships among genotyped animals
  • G: Genomic relationship matrix (genotyped animals only)
Elegance: This matrix seamlessly combines information from genotyped and ungenotyped animals in a single analysis
SNP Effects Derivation
From Animal Effects to SNP Effects:
ag = Zu
û = λDZ'G*-1âg
Where:
  • Z: Matrix relating SNP genotypes to animals
  • u: Vector of SNP effects
  • D: Diagonal matrix of SNP variance weights
  • λ: Variance ratio = σ²u/σ²a
Innovation: Best linear unbiased predictor of SNP effects given animal effects
Iterative Weighting Algorithm
Two Implementation Scenarios
Scenario S1 (Recommended):
  • Compute animal effects once with ssGBLUP
  • Update SNP weights iteratively
  • More stable and consistent
  • Suitable for multi-trait models
Scenario S2 (Experimental):
  • Re-compute both animal and SNP effects each iteration
  • Potentially higher accuracy initially
  • May deteriorate with excessive iterations
  • Risk of overfitting to larger effects
Weight Calculation Strategy
Individual SNP Variance Estimation:
σ̂²u,i = û²i / [2pi(1-pi)]
Normalization Step:
D(t+1) = [tr(D(0)) / tr(D(t+1)*)] × D(t+1)*
Why This Works:
  • Allows unequal variances like BayesB
  • No sampling - deterministic approach
  • Computationally efficient
  • Maintains compatibility with infinitesimal model
Convergence Properties
Optimal Stopping:
  • S1: Stable after 2-3 iterations
  • S2: Best at iteration 2 for m=8 SNPs
  • Further iterations may reduce accuracy
  • Excessive weighting favors large effects
Validation Metrics:
  • Correlation between true and estimated QTL effects
  • Sum of m adjacent SNP effects (m = 1 to 40)
  • Breeding value prediction accuracy
  • Standard deviation across replicates
🧪 Simulation Study Design
1
Population Structure
Complex multi-generational pedigree with realistic breeding structure
Design Parameters:
• 15,800 total animals with phenotypes
• Only 1,500 animals genotyped (9.5%)
• 6 generations (0-5) with complete records
• Heritability = 0.5, phenotypic variance = 1.0
Realistic Challenge:
Mimics livestock breeding programs where most animals have phenotypes but only a subset are genotyped
2
Genomic Architecture
Two chromosomes with realistic QTL and marker distribution
Chromosome Setup:
• Chromosome 1: 1,552 SNPs, 16 QTLs
• Chromosome 2: 1,448 SNPs, 14 QTLs
• Total: 3,000 SNPs, 30 QTLs
• QTL effects from Gamma distribution (shape=0.4, scale=1.36)
Genetic Complexity:
• Minor allele frequency > 0.05
• No overlap between QTL and marker positions
• 1,000 generations of evolution before data collection
3
Method Comparisons
Comprehensive comparison across three major approaches
Methods Tested:
• ssGBLUP with iterative weights (S1 and S2)
• BayesB with deregressed proofs (weighted/unweighted)
• Classical GWAS (CGWAS) using WOMBAT
Evaluation Criteria:
• GEBV accuracy (correlation with true breeding values)
• QTL detection accuracy (correlation with true QTL effects)
• Computational efficiency and robustness

📊 Key Results and Insights
Superior Accuracy and Stability
GEBV Prediction Performance:
  • ssGBLUP achieved 0.89 accuracy vs 0.88 for BayesB
  • Much lower standard deviations across replicates
  • More consistent results than Bayesian methods
  • No need for complex pseudo-data preparation
QTL Detection Power:
  • Optimal window size: 8 adjacent SNPs
  • Peak correlation: 0.82 for ssGBLUP vs 0.83 for BayesB
  • BayesB required 16 SNPs to achieve similar performance
  • CGWAS best for single SNP (m=1) detection
Computational Advantages
Speed Comparison:
  • ssGBLUP: 2 minutes per iteration
  • BayesB: 5 hours with MCMC sampling
  • CGWAS: 13 seconds (but limited functionality)
Practical Benefits:
  • No sampling variance from MCMC
  • Deterministic algorithm
  • Easy to implement and debug
  • Direct use of phenotypes without pseudo-data
Scalability:
  • Linear cost with number of SNPs
  • Cubic cost with genotyped animals (current implementation)
  • Potential for sub-cubic with advanced algorithms
Method-Specific Insights
ssGBLUP Strengths:
  • Best balance of accuracy and stability
  • Utilizes all available phenotypic information
  • Suitable for complex models (multi-trait, maternal effects)
  • No need for deregressed proofs
BayesB Characteristics:
  • Slightly higher peak accuracy
  • Much higher variance across replicates
  • Requires careful tuning and longer runs
  • Limited to genotyped animals only
CGWAS Limitations:
  • Best for single SNP identification
  • Poor at regional QTL detection
  • Noisiest SNP effect estimates
  • Requires separate BLUP run for pseudo-data
Optimal Implementation Strategy
Recommended Protocol:
  • Use Scenario S1: Update weights but not GEBVs iteratively
  • Stop at iteration 2-3: Peak accuracy achieved early
  • Evaluate m=8 SNPs: Optimal window for QTL detection
  • Monitor convergence: Watch for deterioration in later rounds
When to Choose ssGBLUP:
  • Large numbers of ungenotyped relatives with phenotypes
  • Complex models requiring unified analysis
  • Need for computational efficiency and stability
  • Multi-trait or longitudinal data scenarios

⚠️ Limitations and Considerations
Important Limitations: The method assumes infinitesimal model architecture and may not distinguish well between true associations and linkage disequilibrium effects. Significance testing frameworks need further development for practical GWAS applications.
Methodological Limitations
Infinitesimal Model Assumption:
  • Assumes equal variance for all SNP effects initially
  • May not capture true genetic architecture
  • Less realistic than BayesB variable selection
  • Identical G matrix across all traits
Linkage Disequilibrium Issues:
  • Difficulty separating causation from correlation
  • Strong LD in related populations
  • May not identify true causal variants
  • Regional rather than single-SNP resolution
Statistical Concerns
Significance Testing Gap:
  • No established P-value framework
  • Difficult to define detection thresholds
  • Shrunken estimates complicate inference
  • Multiple testing correction unclear
Weight Estimation Issues:
  • Treats SNP effect estimates as true values
  • Ignores uncertainty in effect estimation
  • May lead to excessive weighting
  • Potential for overfitting in later iterations
Practical Constraints
Data Requirements:
  • Requires comprehensive pedigree information
  • Quality control critical for all data types
  • Population stratification may bias results
  • Missing data handling not fully addressed
Computational Scalability:
  • Current implementation scales cubically
  • Memory intensive for large datasets
  • May require approximations for huge populations
  • Need for specialized computing infrastructure

🎯 Key Takeaways and Lasting Impact
Revolutionary Contribution
The Wang et al. (2012) paper fundamentally changed GWAS methodology by demonstrating how to effectively utilize phenotypic information from ungenotyped relatives. This breakthrough enabled cost-effective association studies in populations with complex family structures, particularly transforming livestock genomics and influencing approaches in human genetics and plant breeding.
Essential Principles Established:
  • Unified Framework: Single analysis combining pedigree and genomic information
  • Information Maximization: Use all available phenotypic data, regardless of genotyping status
  • Computational Efficiency: Deterministic approach avoiding expensive MCMC sampling
  • Practical Implementation: Build on existing mixed model infrastructure
  • Iterative Refinement: Improve SNP effect estimation through weighting
🌐 Transformative Impact on Genomics

This methodology became the foundation for modern genomic association studies in structured populations. By solving the fundamental problem of utilizing ungenotyped relatives, it enabled cost-effective GWAS in livestock, influenced human genetics family studies, and provided a unified framework that continues to evolve with advances in statistical genomics and computational methods.
