Detailed Material and Methods Flow
1
Historical Population Simulation
Create realistic base population with appropriate genetic structure
Duration: 4 × Ne = 400 generations of random mating
Population size: 100 individuals per generation
Purpose: Achieve mutation-drift equilibrium
Mutation rate: 5×10⁻⁵ during historical phase
Result: ~64,000-66,000 segregating loci
2
Genome Architecture Design
Define comprehensive genetic framework for simulation
Chromosomes: 18 pairs, each 1 Morgan in length
Total loci: 1,000,000 base pairs initially simulated
QTLs: 6,000 loci with effects from gamma distribution (shape=0.4)
SNP array: 20,000 markers for even genome coverage
MAF filtering: Remove loci with frequency <0.02
3
Breeding Program Structure
Establish realistic pig breeding scheme
Selection candidates: 1,200 males + 1,200 females per generation
Selected parents: 40 males + 400 females per generation
Mating design: Each male mated to 10 females (fully nested)
Litter structure: 6 piglets per litter, same sire
Commercial selection: Top 80 males as AI boars for market
4
Training Population Scenarios
Test effect of genomic prediction accuracy on MSV criteria
Scenario A: Current generation only (lowest accuracy)
Scenario B: Current + past 2 generations (medium accuracy)
Scenario C: Current + past 5 generations (highest accuracy)
Purpose: Investigate impact of prediction accuracy on selection decisions
5
Burn-in Period
Establish Bulmer equilibrium before testing
Duration: 10 generations of genomic selection
Selection method: Standard GEBV-based truncation selection
Purpose: Reach equilibrium variance levels before comparison
Population structure: Maintain consistent breeding program parameters
6
Trait Simulation
Model realistic quantitative trait architecture
Trait: Daily gain (moderate heritability = 0.3)
Phenotyping: All animals measured before selection
Genotyping: All animals genotyped before breeding value estimation
Error structure: Environmental effects added to true breeding values
Model Comparison
Model 1: Standard GEBV Selection
Approach: Traditional genomic prediction
y = μ + Xβ + Zu + e
Components:
• y = phenotype vector
• μ = overall mean
• u = random genomic effects
• Z = genotype matrix
• e = residual error

Assumptions:
• u ~ N(0, Gσ²ᵤ)
• e ~ N(0, Iσ²ₑ)

Selection: Truncation on GEBV
Model 2: Index5 (Current MSV)
Step 1: Calculate gametic MSV for each animal
σ²MSV = Σ pi(1-pi)α²i
Step 2: Combine with breeding value
Index5 = BVanimal + xm × √(2×σ²MSV)
Selection: Rank by Index5 value
Planning horizon: Current generation MSV
Model 3: ExpBVSelGrOff (Two Gen Ahead)
Approach: Maximize selected grand-offspring performance
ExpBVSelGrOff = (BVₛ+BVᴅ)/2 + iᵢ√(σ²Fs + √2iₘ√((1-kᵢ)σ²Fs/4 + σ²ₘsᵥₒff))
Components:
• Parent average breeding values
• Full-sib variance (σ²Fs)
• Selection intensities (iᵢ, iₘ)
• Offspring MSV predictions
7
Selection Algorithm
Novel GCA-inspired approach for complex criteria
Challenge: ExpBVSelGrOff expresses values for pairs/quartets, not individuals
Solution: Iteratively reduce candidates based on average criterion values
Testers: Limited number of mates to compute averages
Scaling: Progressive increase in testers as candidates decrease
8
Validation Design
Comprehensive evaluation across multiple scenarios and metrics
Replicates: 100 per scenario for robust statistical inference
Duration: 19 generations of selection following burn-in
Criteria tested: Random, GEBV, Index5, ExpBVSelGrOff, ExpBVSelGrGrOff
Software: MoBPS version 1.10.68 + R version 4.1.2
Computing: High-performance cluster Anunna
9
Evaluation Metrics
Comprehensive assessment across gain, diversity, and accuracy
Genetic Progress: Population average + commercial genetic level
Genetic Diversity: Genic variance, genetic variance, beneficial alleles lost, inbreeding
Accuracy Measures: GEBV accuracy, MSV prediction accuracy, selection overlap
Implementation: Variance ratios, computational requirements
Why, How, What Analysis
WHY - Problem Statement
Main Issue: Current genomic selection maximizes short-term gain but may compromise long-term sustainability.
  • GEBV selection focuses only on mean performance, ignoring genetic variance
  • Intensive selection reduces genetic diversity over time
  • Risk of genetic plateaus as variance decreases
  • Commercial programs need animals that produce variable, high-performing offspring
  • Previous MSV research assumed known QTL effects, not realistic for practice
Gap: No empirical testing of MSV criteria with estimated SNP effects in large breeding programs.
HOW - Methodology Innovation
Key Innovation 1: Testing MSV criteria with estimated (not assumed) SNP effects.
  • Realistic conditions: First study with estimated marker effects
  • Multiple accuracies: Three training population scenarios
  • Novel algorithm: GCA-inspired selection for complex criteria
  • Forward planning: Criteria planning 1-3 generations ahead
Innovation 2: Comprehensive evaluation framework across genetic gain, diversity, and practical implementation metrics.
WHAT - Key Findings
Results: MSV-considering criteria outperform or equal GEBV selection across all metrics.
  • 2% higher commercial genetic level with longest planning horizon
  • Up to 20% more genetic variance retained
  • Fewer beneficial alleles lost from population
  • Never worse performance than GEBV selection
  • Benefits scale with prediction accuracy and planning horizon
Impact: Enables sustainable breeding with maintained diversity and improved long-term gain potential.
